Introduce the Big Question

What makes you feel proud?

Write the Big Question on chart paper and have students brainstorm ideas about what makes them feel proud.” Use a chart like the one below to capture students’ thoughts and ideas. Tell students they will continue to explore this question and add ideas to the class chart as they read The Proudest Blue: A story of Hijab and Family.*

Display the chart throughout the lesson for students to return to. Students may also complete their own charts and add new ideas throughout the lesson.

What Makes You Feel Proud?
at home at school in your community
  • when I help my sister with
    her homework and she gets a good grade
  • ?
  • when I figure out a hard math problem
  • ?
  • when I work with friends to help clean up a park
  • ?

After brainstorming ideas as a class, have students discuss with a partner. Provide the following sentence frames to support discussion:

At home, I feel proud when ___________ .

At school, I feel proud when ___________ .

In my community, when I ___________ , I feel proud.

* As needed, clarify the meaning of “proud” using examples from your own life. For English learners, you can use facial expressions and pantomime to reinforce understanding. You may also want to point out that the superlative, “proudest,” means “the most proud.”

Summary

Faizah and older sister Asiya shop with Mama for a new hijab for Asiya. Tomorrow is the sisters’ first day back to school. For the first time, sixth grader Asiya will wear hijab at school, and Faizah is thrilled with Asiya’s choice of a beautiful, blue hijab. The next day, Faizah feels special with her new backpack and light-up shoes. When she sees Asiya in her first-day hijab, Faizah beams with pride. Arriving at school, Asiya leaves Faizah with the younger children and joins the sixth graders. Faizah is full of hope and excitement, but when a classmate asks her what her sister has on her head, Faizah timidly whispers an answer. Faizah catches herself, imagining that her sister’s hijab “isn’t a whisper,” but blue and wonderful as the sky. She recalls her mother’s words that the first day of wearing hijab is important. “It means being strong.”

REMINDER: This book does not include page numbers. This section starts on page 2, beginning with the words “Mama holds out the pink” and ends on page 11 with the words, “It means being strong.”

Summary

Feeling unsure of herself, Faizah runs to give Asiya a hug. Suddenly the sisters hear laughter. A boy is pointing at Asiya’s hijab. A confused Faizah asks, “Why?” She recalls her mother’s message that while some people will not understand the hijab, “if you understand who you are, one day they will too.” Later in class, Faizah draws a picture of two princesses in blue hijabs—her and Asiya. A classmate admires Faizah’s drawing. At recess, Faizah is back to feeling hopeful and excited. But once again, taunts aimed at Asiya upset Faizah. Asiya and her friends ignore the bullies. Faizah recalls more of her mother’s words, encouraging her not to hold on to hurtful words. These words, and seeing Asiya’s example, give Faizah the strength to walk away, too.

Note: See About the Book for potential sensitivity issues about bullying and Islamophobia.

Mirrors & Windows

Responding to Bullies

The two episodes of bullying in this section show Faizah’s confusion in the face of intolerance and how Asiya’s sense of self and inner strength help both sisters deal with it. Guide students to compare and contrast the sisters’ reactions. Then have students reflect on their own feelings about bullying. Prompt discussion with guiding questions such as the following:

-How does Faizah feel when she first hears the bully laughing at Asiya? Why? (Faizah doesn’t understand it; she can’t understand why others don’t see Asiya’s hijab the same way she does—proud and beautiful.)
-How are Faizah’s reactions to the bullying different from Asiya’s? (Asiya and her friends calmly go back to playing tag; Faizah gets mad.)
-What might Faizah learn from Asiya about dealing with bullies? How does feeling proud help both girls? (Faizah learns to stay proud of herself and ignore the bullies. This helps both Asiya and Faizah let go of the hurtful words.)
-Have you ever had to overcome a big challenge like this? How did it make you feel about yourself?

You might share situations in your life where your own pride or sense of self helped you overcome a difficult situation. For example, “When I was in fifth grade, classmates made fun of me because they thought my singing sounded funny. I ignored them and just sang louder and prouder!”

Plot Overview

Faizah and older sister Asiya accompany their mother to the hijab store to pick out Asiya’s first-day hijab. The next day is the girls’ first day back to school, and it will be sixth grader Asiya’s first time wearing hijab at school. Asiya picks a beautiful blue hijab, which Faizah admires. The next day, the sisters arrive at school—Faizah feeling special with a new backpack and light-up shoes, and Asiya wearing her “proudest blue” hijab.

When school-yard bullies hurl insults at Asiya, the “proudest blue” of her hijab gives her the strength and sense of self to ignore the hurtful words. Asiya’s resilience comforts a confused and upset Faizah, who sees in Asiya’s actions a model for dealing with intolerance.

Theme Overview

Hijab and Pride in Self, Family, and Culture In this story, hijab is a symbol of cultural and familial pride—a source of strength for both sisters. Hijab is a traditional scarf, or head covering, worn by many Muslim women around the world. Exact practices, including the age when women begin wearing hijab, vary among different groups, often by region, sect, or family tradition. The author’s note provides more details on the practice, including Ibtihaj Muhammad’s personal experience wearing hijab. Muhammad’s cultural and religious pride are reflected throughout the story, as Faizah recalls their mother’s words of wisdom, reinforcing lessons of dignity and pride.

Meeting Bullying with Resilience Asiya encounters taunts about her hijab from faceless school-yard bullies. The younger Faizah witnesses the bullying, which confuses, angers, and scares her. Asiya, however, ignores the bullying with quiet resilience, and proves to be a role model for her sister. The “proudest blue” hijab is a source of strength for both girls as they learn to be resilient in the face of prejudice and ignorance.

Sensitivity Issues

Bullying The story’s central conflict is bullying by other students in a school playground. The illustrations depict the bullies as faceless, dark forms. Be extra sensitive to students’ reactions, and keep in mind that some of your students may have experienced or witnessed bullying. Muslim students in particular may have strong reactions to the events, as one of two Muslim sisters is taunted for wearing hijab at school. For some insight into dealing with this issue, see the article from LearningforJustice.org, which shares one teacher’s experience dealing with Islamophobia in the classroom.

To locate the article, copy and paste the URL into your browser:

https://www.learningforjustice.org/magazine/confronting-students-islamophobia

Finally, note that most of the bully figures in the illustrations are suggestive of male shapes, and Faizah refers to the bullies as “boys.” Some readers may conclude that most bullying is done by boys, which could be seen as a biased viewpoint on intolerance and bullying.

Activity Support

Four Corners: What Makes You Feel Proud?

The Four Corners activity asks students to reflect on different examples of things that make people feel proud. Use this activity as a warm-up for exploring ideas of pride in The Proudest Blue: A Story of Hijab and Family.

1. Post one of the four examples in each corner of the room. Include a visual for each example.

  • helping to make a special food for a family or community celebration
  • working hard to create a beautiful drawing in art class
  • watching a friend or family member play well in a sport
  • supporting a friend who is being picked on by bullies

2. Read aloud each example and tell students to think about how it reflects (or does not reflect) things that make them feel proud. Have students discuss each example with a partner. Provide the following sentence frame to support discussion:
This would / would not make me feel proud because ___________ .

3. After discussing each example in pairs, tell students to move to the corner of the room displaying the example that would make them feel the proudest. Remind students that “proudest” means “the most proud.”

4. Give students time to discuss the example they chose in groups. Encourage students to share similar situations that made them proud. Provide the following sentence frames to support discussion:

This example would make me feel the proudest because ___________ .

It reminds me of a time in my life when ___________ .

5. Have a student from each group summarize their group’s discussion.

Activity Support

Point of View

Write “Point of View” on chart paper and explain that this refers to who is telling the story. Explain that the person who tells a story is called the narrator. Clues in the text such as pronouns I, me, and we help you identify the narrator. List the boldfaced terms on the chart paper.

1. Read aloud the opening two pages of the story. Ask students to identify the three main characters—Asiya, Faizah, and Mama. Then have students think about the following questions:

  • Which character is telling the story?
  • What clues in the text or the illustrations help you know which character is telling the story?

2. Once students have had time to think, have them turn and talk about their ideas with a partner. After a few minutes, have pairs of students share their ideas with the whole class.

3. Summarize the following points, adding the story details to the chart paper:

  • Faizah is the narrator.
  • The story is told from Faizah’s point of view—it tells her thoughts and feelings about the events and other characters.
  • Clues in the text such as “I know why,” “I know it,” and “We’re sisters” help readers know that Faizah is the narrator.

4. Display the chart paper as an anchor chart throughout the lesson and have students refer and add to it as they read.

5. Finally, tell students to think about why the author chose to make the younger sister the narrator as they read the story.

Figurative Language: Simile, Metaphor, and Personification

On the chart/paper, display the sample sentences in a table such as the following. Underline the words “like,” “tiger,” and “yelled” as shown. Leave extra space in each row below the examples to show where students will write additional examples from the text as they read the story.

  • Josh’s alarm clock yelled at him to wake up.

1. Have students think about the example sentences, and then turn and talk about them with a partner. Use the following questions to prompt discussion.

  • What images come to mind when you read these sentences?
  • What do the descriptions make you wonder about Josh?
  • What kind of comparisons do these examples make?

2. After pairs discuss their ideas, facilitate a whole class discussion. Use the examples in the table to review the following key ideas:

  • Figurative language uses comparisons to describe something.
  • Similes use the words “like” or “as” ; metaphors and personification do not.
  • Similes and metaphors compare things that have similar qualities.
  • Personification gives qualities of people to things that are not people.

3. Point out that in figurative language the meaning is not literal (Josh is not really a tiger). Figurative language compares two or more things because they share similar qualities (Josh plays sports with the quickness of a tiger).

4. Display the table on the chart/paper as an anchor chart throughout the lesson and have students refer and add to it as they read.

Unlock a Key Sentence

Start with the sentence chunk that tells who or what the sentence is mostly about. Continue to unpack meaning and connect ideas, chunk by chunk, using the guiding questions in the table below.

  But | if you understand who you are | one day | they will too.”

Sentence Chunk Discuss Meaning
if you understand who you are -What is this sentence about? (understanding who you are)
-Who is the speaker? (Mama)
-Who is the “you” Mama is speaking to in this sentence? (Asiya and Faizah)
-What does Mama want Faizah and Asiya to understand? (who they are)
-What does it mean to understand “who you are”? (knowing yourself—what you like, what you think is right and wrong, what you are proud of)
-Why does the author use the word “if”? (to indicate a relationship between ideas)
-Who else could the “you” be in this sentence? (the reader, all of us)
they will too -Who are “they” in this sentence? (people who do not understand the hijab)

one day -When will this new understanding happen? (one day)
-What does “one day” mean? (a time in the future)
But -Why does the author begin the sentence with the word “but”? (to signal a different way of thinking)

Unlock a Key Sentence

Display and read aloud the key sentence. Then guide students in unlocking its meaning by discussing each sentence chunk using the protocol described below.

Start with the sentence chunk that tells who or what the sentence is mostly about. Continue to unpack meaning and connect ideas, chunk by chunk, using the guiding questions in the table below..

“It is a valiant and agonizing struggle | to focus. | not on what you have lost . . . but | on what you have been given.”

Sentence Chunk Discuss Meaning
It is a valiant and agonizing struggle -What is this sentence mostly about? (a struggle)
-What is a struggle? (a challenge, difficulty, or fight)
-What kind of struggle is it? (valiant and agonizing)
-What does valiant mean? Point out the Spanish cognate valiant for Spanish-speaking students. (brave, heroic)
-What does agonizing mean? (painful, difficult)
-What does it mean if a struggle is both valiant and agonizing? (It is difficult but heroic; it is worth the challenge.)
to focus -What does to focus mean? (pay attention to, concentrate on)
-What does the sentence say to focus on? (We don’t know yet; let’s look in the next chunks.)
on what you have been given -What does this chunk tell us about “focus”? (This is what to focus on: what you have been given.) -What kinds of “gifts” might the authors want people to focus on? (gifts such as love, kindness, faith, school, and support from friends and family)
not on what you have lost…but -What does this chunk tell us about “focus”? (This is what not to focus on: what you have lost.)
-Why might a refugee focus on what he or she has lost? (Refugees often lose so much: their homes, family members, friends, school, jobs, and toys and other belongings. It can be hard to focus on anything else.)
-How do not and but work together in this sentence? (They show a contrasting relationship between two ideas: not this, but that.)
-What ideas are being contrasted in this sentence? (Ideas about what to focus on: not what you have lost but what you have been given.)
-Why might this be a good message for everyone to focus on? (It helps us to appreciate what we have and not dwell on what we do not.)

After unlocking the key sentence, have students craft their own sentence using the following sentence frame:

It is a/an __________ struggle to focus not on __________ but on __________ .

Activity Support

Author’s Craft: Personification

  • 1. Discuss students’ examples of non-human movie or TV characters with human-like qualities. Some suggestions: a rat that can talk, read recipes, and cook in a kitchen; a teakettle or candlestick holder that can sing.   • 2. Ask students to describe how the characters acted like humans. What human qualities did they have?

  • 7. Now have students write a sentence that uses personification to describe Asiya’s hijab to show that it is special.
